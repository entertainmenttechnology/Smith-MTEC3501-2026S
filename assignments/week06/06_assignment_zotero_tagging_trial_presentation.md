# Part II – Practice Presentation (March 9, Room V103B)

This is a **practice run** of your March 16 juried presentation.

It is not expected to be polished.

It **must be specific.**

If something is still unresolved, you must clearly state:

- What is unclear
- What assumption remains unproven
- What research or test is required

Important:
If you are stuck between two choices, do not leave the section blank.

Instead:

- State the two options explicitly
- Identify the decision criteria
- Describe what test or research will resolve it
- Place the open question directly into your process diagram

At this stage, it is better to document a structured question than to leave a structural gap.

An unresolved but articulated decision can be assessed.
A blank space cannot.

Vagueness is not acceptable.

# Practice Presentation Structure (March 9, in-person)

Use the following structure (adapted from the official deliverables document fileciteturn4file0):

### 1. Introduction (1–2 minutes)

- Project Name & One-Sentence Summary
- Project Type (game, installation, animation, AI tool, etc.)
- Goal of the project and why it matters

### 2. Research & Precedents (2–3 minutes)

- Key references and inspirations
- Technical or artistic precedents
- Relevant prior work (if applicable)

### 3. Project Breakdown (3–4 minutes)

- Climax Version (full vision)
- Prototype Plan (what your end-of-semester PoC will demonstrate)
- Technical requirements & tools
- Expected challenges or uncertainties

### 4. Milestones & Feasibility (2 minutes)

- GitHub milestone structure (Research, Design, Produce, Publish, Assess)
- Timeline and development strategy

### 5. Questions for Feedback (Include All Current Unknowns)

- Areas needing clarification
- Structural uncertainties
- Scope or feasibility concerns
- Explicit unknowns and unproven assumptions

You must clearly identify where your project is still unresolved.
If something is not yet solved, name it directly.

Timing requirement:
Stay under 10 minutes to allow critique.

# Part III – Process Diagram (Specificity & Breakpoint Integration)

This diagram must directly emerge from your **Make It Specific** document and your identified breakpoints ("miracles").

You must create a **flowchart-style process diagram** that demonstrates:

- Each step required for the system to be built
- The transformation between stages
- Where former breakpoints have been decomposed
- Remaining structural uncertainties (if any)
- The path leading to your end-of-semester PoC

This is not conceptual.
This is procedural.

The diagram must:

- Be sequential and logically ordered
- Show dependencies between steps
- Identify where research informs action
- Replace any large jump with smaller, explicit steps

If a large leap remains in the diagram, that leap is still a miracle.

Acceptable format:

- Flowchart (preferred)

The diagram must appear in your slide deck.

# Best Practices – Oral Presentation

### Do:

- Speak clearly and deliberately
- Define technical terms
- Use diagrams to explain systems
- Identify risks honestly
- Stay within time limit

### Do Not:

- Read from slides
- Overload slides with text
- Use vague adjectives ("immersive," "innovative," "engaging") without mechanism
- Skip breakpoints
- Ignore feasibility

Your presentation should sound like an engineering review, not a marketing pitch.

---

# Best Practices – Slide Design

### Slides Should:

- Contain minimal text
- Use diagrams instead of paragraphs
- Highlight structure and transformation
- Separate PoC, LVP, and Climax clearly

### Slides Should Not:

- Contain dense bullet lists
- Duplicate your spoken script
- Include decorative imagery without structural relevance

If a slide can stand alone without you speaking, it is probably too dense.

# Presentation Discipline – Critical Rule

DO NOT READ FROM THE SLIDES.

This does NOT mean you cannot read your prepared talk.

It means:

- Do not place your script on the slide
- Do not overload slides with paragraphs
- Do not treat slides as cue cards

If you need support:

- Use speaker notes (if available)
- Use a printed outline
- Use a separate document

Slides should show structure, diagrams, and key anchors — not your spoken text.
